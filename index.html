Abstract

This webpage accompanies our paper, "Review Paper on Algorithms and Advancements in Detecting Deepfakes." We synthesize recent progress in automated deepfake detection, comparing traditional machine learning with deep learning approaches, discussing dataset biases (e.g., FF++ prevalence) and generalization gaps on challenging benchmarks like DFDC. We provide a visual summary, a poster, and a video overview.

Datasets & Benchmarks

We summarize the benchmark datasets most frequently used in the reviewed studies and their implications for model generalization. Overreliance on FF++ often inflates reported accuracy, while DFDC presents more realistic challenges due to greater diversity in compression, lighting, and demographics.

Interpreting performance requires cross-dataset evaluation; models that excel on FF++ may drop substantially on DFDC.

Objectives & Research Questions

We summarize our goals and guiding questions to frame the review and its evaluation criteria.

  • Analyze the effectiveness of current machine learning approaches in detecting deepfakes and synthetic accounts.
  • Examine and compare reference datasets used to train and evaluate detectors, identifying suitability and gaps.
  • Investigate ethical implications of automated verification systems, including privacy and algorithmic biases.

The Problem

The exponential growth of generative technologies has radically transformed the digital media landscape. Deep learning tools like GANs and modern LLMs enable hyperrealistic synthetic content (deepfakes) that can be weaponized for misinformation and fraud. According to DeepStrike (2025), deepfake files surged from 500,000 in 2023 to over 8 million in 2025 (+1,500%), while deepfake-related fraud attempts rose by 3,000% in 2023 (including +1,740% in North America). Human detection of high-quality manipulations is critically low (~24.5%), underscoring the need for automated detectors and standardized evaluation.

Ecuadorian context: official bulletins report school incidents where students used AI to generate sexualized images of peers, constituting child sexual content and cyberbullying. In 2024, authorities recorded 8,724 ICT-related crime notices, including 1,030 electronic fraud, 105 computer falsification, 118 child pornography cases, and 105 cases where criminals exploited deepfakes (identity theft, fraudulent calls). Reports also warn that criminal groups deploy deepfakes to impersonate victims, extort, and simulate kidnappings—deceiving families of missing migrants. These trends highlight both social harm and the urgency for robust, generalizable detection.

Methodology

We performed a systematic literature review following PRISMA guidelines. We searched across Google Scholar, arXiv, Springer, and IEEE, identifying 50 papers published in the last 5 years. After applying screening and eligibility criteria, 30 high-quality papers were included for final analysis.
